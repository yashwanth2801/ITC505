A Lover's Complaint

FROM off a hill whose concave womb reworded
A plaintful story from a sistering vale,
My spirits to attend this double voice accorded,
And down I laid to list the sad-tuned tale;
Ere long espied a fickle maid full pale,
Tearing of papers, breaking rings a-twain,
Storming her world with sorrow's wind and rain.
Upon her head a platted hive of straw,
Which fortified her visage from the sun,
Whereon the thought might think sometime it saw
The carcass of beauty spent and done:
Time had not scythed all that youth begun,
Nor youth all quit; but, spite of heaven's fell rage,
Some beauty peep'd through lattice of sear'd age.
Oft did she heave her napkin to her eyne,
Which on it had conceited characters,
Laundering the silken figures in the brine
That season'd woe had pelleted in tears,
And often reading what contents it bears;
As often shrieking undistinguish'd woe,
In clamours of all size, both high and low.
Sometimes her levell'd eyes their carriage ride,
As they did battery to the spheres intend;
Sometime diverted their poor balls are tied
To the orbed earth; sometimes they do extend
Their view right on; anon their gazes lend
To every place at once, and, nowhere fix'd,
The mind and sight distractedly commix'd.
Her hair, nor loose nor tied in formal plat,
Proclaim'd in her a careless hand of pride
For some, untuck'd, descended her sheaved hat,
Hanging her pale and pined cheek beside;
Some in her threaden fillet still did bide,
And true to bondage would not break from thence,
Though slackly braided in loose negligence.
A thousand favours from a maund she drew
Of amber, crystal, and of beaded jet,
Which one by one she in a river threw,
Upon whose weeping margent she was set;
Like usury, applying wet to wet,
Or monarch's hands that let not bounty fall
Where want cries some, but where excess begs all.
Of folded schedules had she many a one,
Which she perused, sigh'd, tore, and gave the flood;
Crack'd many a ring of posied gold and bone
Bidding them find their sepulchres in mud;
Found yet moe letters sadly penn'd in blood,
With sleided silk feat and affectedly
Enswathed, and seal'd to curious secrecy.
These often bathed she in her fluxive eyes,
And often kiss'd, and often 'gan to tear:
Cried 'O false blood, thou register of lies,
What unapproved witness dost thou bear!
Ink would have seem'd more black and damned here!'
This said, in top of rage the lines she rents,
Big discontent so breaking their contents.
A reverend man that grazed his cattle nigh--
Sometime a blusterer, that the ruffle knew
Of court, of city, and had let go by
The swiftest hours, observed as they flew--
Towards this afflicted fancy fastly drew,
And, privileged by age, desires to know
In brief the grounds and motives of her woe.
So slides he down upon his grained bat,
And comely-distant sits he by her side;
When he again desires her, being sat,
Her grievance with his hearing to divide:
If that from him there may be aught applied
Which may her suffering ecstasy assuage,
'Tis promised in the charity of age.
'Father,' she says, 'though in me you behold
The injury of many a blasting hour,
Let it not tell your judgment I am old;
Not age, but sorrow, over me hath power:
I might as yet have been a spreading flower,
Fresh to myself, If I had self-applied
Love to myself and to no love beside.

Addendum

The page consists of two sections through my implementation of article because I needed to separate the poem content from the explanatory text.

  • Use of article: The article tag separates the poem and explanation into distinct sections, helping keep the content organized and easy to understand.
  • Use of blockquote: The blockquote tag indicates that the poem is a quotation, distinguishing it from the rest of the page content.
  • Use of p tags: Each line of the poem has its own p tag instead of using br for line breaks. This improves text clarity and structure.
  • Use of footer: The footer tag shows the "Last updated" time using JavaScript. It automatically updates each time the page is loaded.
  • Use of h1 and h2: The main title is formatted as an ;h1element, while the addendum uses an h2element for the subheading. This helps present the content clearly.
  • Use of ul and li: I used ul and li to create an organized list that makes the explanation of the HTML choices easy to follow.
  • Improving Accessibility: The use of these HTML tags helps make the page easier to read for everyone, including people with disabilities, making the page more universally accessible.

    • I will clarify all the styling choices I made in the CSS file that affects the page presentation. I will explain each decision for choosing fonts and sizes and selecting colors along with margin and padding values to enhance page appearance while improving readability.

    Font Choices

    The main text appearance uses Georgia as its primary font choice for the document. The serif font creates elegant classical styles that match the essay content effectively. Serif fonts create better readability in extended passages and this fact led me to use them throughout the main text section of the page. The page headings display 'Times New Roman' as their font because this standard text element creates a professional formal visual effect.

    Heading and Text Size

    At 36px the main heading (h1) emerges prominently in order to establish proper visual ranking relative to other page elements. The second-level header subheading (h2) displays text at 28px creating sufficient readability while being easily distinguishable from the top-level header. The text body displays at 18px since this ensures optimal readability regardless of screen size.

    Color Choices

    The page background has a light gray tone of #f4f4f4 that both reduces eye fatigue and makes text more visible. The dark gray text color #333 serves as the main text representation to ensure high readability due to its strong contrast against other elements. The page maintains a professional yet non-jarring color scheme through the headings' use of dark colors #2c3e50 and #34495e. The blockquote section uses #f9f9f9 as its soft background color to create visual distinction from standard text.

    Margin and Padding Choices

    The article tag received 20 pixels of padding in order to establish visual space throughout its text content. A 20px padding element applied to the article tag produces aesthetic appeal by keeping text contents off the container edges. A left margin of 40px creates an organized system of list items for the ul elements found within the addendum section. The page features both 0 margin and padding to prevent superfluous white spaces from manifesting anywhere on the page.

    HTML Abuse

    The elements received HTML corrections in this project since I selected some HTML components mainly for their visual appeal instead of structural accuracy. The title of my previous Lab 2 essay used an h2 element because the font size seemed appealing yet this version utilized an h1 tag for the main title since it represents the highest-level heading in the page.
